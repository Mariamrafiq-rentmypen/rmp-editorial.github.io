Sunday at Nine

Memoir Sample · Grief & Family

The phone rings at exactly nine. UK time. I know without looking that it is her, because she has called at nine every Sunday for six years, ever since I left, and the only thing that has ever interrupted the pattern was the hospital.

"Beta, khaya kuch?" Have you eaten something.

I am standing at my kitchen counter in Birmingham with a mug of tea going lukewarm in my hand, and I say yes, I have eaten, even though I have not, because saying no invites a conversation I do not have the energy for on a Sunday morning. She tells me Aunty Shireen's son got a promotion in London. Finance. Very well settled. I say that is wonderful. She tells me the neighbor's daughter is getting married in October, the one with the loud laugh, do I remember her. I do not remember her. I say yes.

This is what our calls were. Twelve minutes, sometimes fifteen if there was a wedding to discuss. She never once asked if I was happy. Not in six years. I used to think this was because she did not care to know, that the question itself was somehow beneath her, too soft for a woman who ran a household through a civil war's worth of relatives and never once raised her voice doing it...

What the Fixing Looks Like

Memoir Sample · Military & Identity

When my wife was pregnant with our daughter, I did not sleep much during the last few months, and it had nothing to do with the usual reasons new fathers lose sleep. I kept thinking about the moment the nurse would hand her to me for the first time, and what her small unformed eyes would do when they found my face.

Newborns cannot see much in the first weeks, I knew that, I had read enough to know that, but I could not shake the fear anyway, the picture of her flinching, or worse, the picture of her simply never learning to read my face the way other children read their fathers' faces, some permanent flatness in our bond that I would have caused just by looking the way I look.

I told Sarah some of this, not all of it, the night before her due date. She took my hand, the burned one, the one with the grafted skin along the back of it, and held it against her stomach.

"She already knows your voice," she said. "She has known it for months. That is the part that matters first."
